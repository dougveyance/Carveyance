Ghost Run

In the style of John Frankenheimer’s Ronin
Written in the tradition of Michael Mann’s Heat and the work of John Hodge

An Original Screenplay In the style of John Frankenheimer’s RONIN

Written in the tradition of Michael Mann’s HEAT and the work of John Hodge

THE MACHINES

Before the people, the cars. Because in this story the cars are not props. They are extensions of the people inside them. They have personalities. They have histories. They have limits — and the whole question of the next ninety minutes is whose limits get found first.

THE LOTUS CARLTON

1990. Racing green so dark it reads as black in shadow. A Vauxhall Carlton bodied saloon that Lotus Engineering took apart and rebuilt as something the factory never intended. 3.6-litre twin-turbocharged straight-six. 377 brake horsepower. 419 lb-ft of torque arriving at 4,200 RPM in a wave that presses you flat. At the time of its release it was the fastest production saloon car in the world. It looked like a company director’s car and went like a Le Mans prototype. That contradiction is the whole point. It is Cole’s car because it is Cole — ordinary surface, extraordinary interior, dangerous when provoked.

THE JAGUAR XJR (x2)

1998. Tempest grey. The XJR is the performance variant of the XJ8 — a 4.0-litre supercharged AJ-V8, 375 brake horsepower, 387 lb-ft of torque available from 3,050 RPM and building all the way to the redline. The supercharger — an Eaton M90 Roots-type blower sitting atop the engine — produces a distinctive pressurised whine that rises in pitch with throttle demand, layering over the baritone V8 note like a second instrument. It weighs 1,835 kilograms. It is large, authoritative, and fast in the way that expensive things are fast — effortlessly, without drama, which makes it more frightening, not less. Shaw drives the lead car. It suits him.

THE LANCIA DELTA HF INTEGRALE EVOLUZIONE

1992. Matte black. No badging. The

Integrale Evo is a homologation special — a car built to satisfy rally regulations, which means it was designed to be driven very fast on surfaces that would destroy most cars. Turbocharged 2.0-litre four-cylinder, 210 brake horsepower, permanent four-wheel drive through a Torsen centre differential that distributes torque between axles in real time, reading the road 400 times per second. It weighs 1,270 kilograms. It is 162 centimetres wide. It can go places the Carlton and the XJRs cannot. This is why Rabbit drives it. This is why they call him Rabbit.

CHARACTERS

COLEMid-40s. Lean, weathered. Eyes that have seen the inside of a cell for four years and haven’t forgotten a single day. He drives like he thinks — clean, economical, no wasted motion. He owes a debt he didn’t earn. The Lotus Carlton is his instrument.
NADIALate 30s. Cole’s navigator and intelligence. French-Algerian. Former Interpol analyst who crossed to the other side for reasons she keeps close. She reads maps the way other people read people — instinctively, ahead of what’s actually there.
RABBITEarly 30s. Cole’s third — running parallel, separate, connected by earpiece. Young. Too eager about some things and not eager enough about others. He memorised every detail of the bank job before Cole had finished the briefing. He drives the Integrale the way you drive something you understand completely — which is to say, right at the limit, all the time, on purpose.
SHAWLate 40s. Cole’s former partner. The one who made the call that put Cole away. He tells himself it was business. He’s been telling himself that for four years. He drives the lead XJR like a man trying to outrun something that’s sitting right next to him.
CONNELLY40s. Shaw’s right-hand. Thick-necked, precise. Loyal in the way guard dogs are loyal — not from love but from training.
PETRA30s. Shaw’s rear-seat intelligence. She speaks four languages and hates every person in the car with her. She has the contract. She decides who gets paid.

ACT ONE: THE CITY

EXT. LYON, FRANCE — RUE MERCIÈRE — EARLY MORNING — ESTABLISHING

The old quarter of Lyon. Pre-dawn. The Saône moves black and slow beyond the stone embankments. Street cleaners push wet bristles across cobblestones that haven’t changed since Napoleon. A cat crosses a yellow square of light from a bakery beginning its morning. Nobody else is awake.

A LOTUS CARLTON sits idling in a narrow side street off the Rue Mercière. Racing green — darker than forest, almost black in this light. The 3.6-litre twin-turbocharged straight-six ticks quietly at idle — 800 RPM — barely breathing. A coiled thing.

CAMERA — LOW AND TIGHT on the exhaust pipe. A thin thread of condensation rising in the cold air. The engine note: a low, complex thrum, not quite settled, holding its breath.

Inside —

INT. LOTUS CARLTON — CONTINUOUS

COLE sits behind the wheel. Still. The engine idles and he idles with it. He has a coffee in a paper cup that he hasn’t drunk from in twenty minutes. On his lap — a hand-drawn schematic. The Banque Centrale du Rhône, three blocks east. Entry points. Guard rotations. Camera arcs. His handwriting is small and precise, the way you write when you’ve learned to compress information into the smallest possible space.

NADIA is beside him, knees drawn up against the dash, a digital camera with a long lens angled through a gap in the window tint. She photographs a delivery truck unloading at the bank’s service entrance. No wasted frames.

In Cole’s right ear — a small earpiece. A channel open.

RABBIT (V.O.)(earpiece — low, steady)I’m on the south side of the Place des Jacobins. Sightline to the main entrance. Guard rotation just happened — six-oh-three, same as yesterday.
COLE(quietly, not moving)Same time both days?
RABBIT (V.O.)Six-oh-three. Exactly.
NADIA(not looking up from the camera)Guards who are exactly on time are either very professional or following a scripted pattern someone designed for them.
RABBIT (V.O.)What’s the difference?
COLEProfessional guards adapt. Scripted patterns have gaps.

A beat.

COLE (CONT’D)Which one is it?

Pause on the earpiece.

RABBIT (V.O.)…I don’t know yet.
COLEThen you’ve timed it once.

Silence. The delivery truck pulls away. Nadia lowers the camera, reviews the shots.

NADIAEastern wall camera has a twelve-second blind spot when it pans. The service door sits in it for nine of those twelve.
COLENine is enough.
NADIANine is exactly enough. Which means it isn’t.

Cole almost smiles. Almost.

COLEThursday. Same time. Different cars.

He reaches to turn the key —

— and stops.

His eyes go to the rearview mirror. CAMERA — CLOSE ON COLE’S EYES in the mirror. Something registers. Very small. The slight constriction of the pupils.

Nothing behind them. Just the empty street, the sodium lights, the long shadows between old stone buildings.

But Cole doesn’t look away.

NADIA(reading him)What?
COLEA car turned off its lights two blocks back.

Silence.

COLE (CONT’D)It turned them off.

He puts the Carlton in first gear. The clutch engages with a precise mechanical click. He pulls away.

EXT. RUE MERCIÈRE — CONTINUOUS

The Carlton rolls forward. Unhurried. Invisible.

WIDE SHOT — the narrow street, the old stone walls, the Carlton moving through it like

something that belongs there.

Two blocks behind, in a pool of shadow beneath a dead streetlamp — a JAGUAR XJR. Tempest grey. Motionless. Its silhouette is long and low and completely without warmth. The kind of car that makes you aware of distances.

Inside —

INT. JAGUAR XJR (LEAD) — CONTINUOUS

SHAW watches the Carlton’s taillights through the windscreen. His hands rest on the rim of the steering wheel. Not gripping. Resting. He studies the distance between the two cars the way a chess player studies a board — measuring combinations, not reacting to the immediate move.

CONNELLY is beside him. Thick arms folded. Waiting.

PETRA sits behind them with a folder open on her lap. She doesn’t look up.

PETRAHe’s made you.
SHAW(quiet)He hasn’t made me.
PETRAThe car just started moving.
SHAWHe was always going to start moving. He’s done his recon.
PETRAHe turned toward the river. That’s not the route back to his hotel.

Shaw says nothing. Watches the Carlton’s distant taillights.

CONNELLYShe’s right.
SHAWI know she’s right.

A long pause. The Carlton’s lights disappear around a corner.

SHAW (CONT’D)(quietly, almost to himself)He always knew when the temperature changed. Before anyone else in the room.
CONNELLYIs that admiration or a warning?
SHAW(reaching for the ignition)Both.

CAMERA — CLOSE ON the XJR’s dashboard as Shaw turns the key. The 4.0-litre supercharged V8 fires — a deep, chest-resonant ignition, followed immediately by the supercharger’s distinctive pressurised undertone, a mechanical whisper beneath the engine

note. A sound like something being compressed. Something building pressure.

The XJR pulls into the empty street.

FROM ABOVE — WIDE — the pre-dawn city. Two cars threading through narrow streets. The Carlton taking the long way, feeling the shape of its tail. The XJR at a disciplined interval — patient.

Behind the XJR, a full block back — a SECOND JAGUAR XJR. Same colour. Same intent. Identical in every way except that the driver is younger, and angrier, and has something to prove.

And three streets west, invisible to all of them — a small black shape moving parallel. The LANCIA INTEGRALE. Rabbit running his own route, his own geometry.

INT. LOTUS CARLTON — MOVING

Cole navigates the narrow streets of the Presqu’île — the peninsula between Lyon’s two rivers — at exactly the pace of someone who has nowhere pressing to be. His eyes work the mirrors in a constant rotation: centre, left, right, centre. Never stopping. Never urgent.

CAMERA — OVER COLE’S SHOULDER, through the windscreen. The narrow street ahead, wet cobblestones reflecting the sodium lights, the occasional parked car pressing in from the sides. This car is wide. 1,785mm. Every gap is calculated.

NADIA(not turning around)Two of them.
COLEI see it.
RABBIT (V.O.)(earpiece)Say again?
COLEWe have a tail. Two cars. Hold your position.
RABBIT (V.O.)Copy. What do you need?
COLENothing yet. Stay loose.

Cole turns left onto the Quai de la Pêcherie. The Saône opens up on their right — wide, dark, the far bank just beginning to show against the lightening sky.

Cole’s jaw tightens. A small, involuntary thing that Nadia catches.

NADIA(watching him)You know them.

It isn’t a question.

Beat.

COLEI know one of them.
NADIAThe job?
COLEThe job. Or they want us not to finish it.
NADIAThere’s a difference.
COLEThere’s a significant difference. And I don’t know which it is yet.

He looks in the mirror. The lead XJR has closed to two hundred metres. Still disciplined. Still patient.

Cole’s hand moves to the gear lever. Settles on it. Not shifting. Just resting. Waiting.

INT. JAGUAR XJR (LEAD) — MOVING

CONNELLYHe’s heading toward the tunnel approach. If he gets to the A6 —
SHAWHe won’t take the A6. Too exposed. Too many cameras.
CONNELLYThen where?

Shaw’s hands tighten on the wheel. Not with uncertainty — with recognition. He knows this man. He knows how he builds a route. He knows the logic.

SHAWThe D383. Out through Caluire, up through the Ain valley. The old route.
CONNELLYHow do you know?

Shaw looks at him. A look that says: because I taught him.

He picks up the radio handset.

SHAW(into radio)Vincent. He’s going to break north. Stay with me through the tunnel, then we spread.

Crackle. The second XJR —

VINCENT (V.O.)(radio, flat)Copy. Don’t lose him in the tunnel. He’ll use the dark.

Shaw sets the radio down. Looks at the Carlton’s taillights ahead — two red points moving through the city like something alive.

His face in the pale morning light is complicated. The face of a man who has been carrying something for a long time and is now close enough to setting it down that he can’t decide if

he wants to.

PETRA(without looking up)For what it’s worth — he’s going to fight.
SHAWI know.
PETRAHe did four years he didn’t deserve.

A beat.

SHAW(very quietly)I know that too.
PETRAThen you know this isn’t going to be clean.

Shaw says nothing. The XJR’s supercharger hums its constant pressurised note. The city moves past. The sky continues to lighten.

ACT TWO: THE TUNNEL

EXT. CALUIRE-ET-CUIRE — TUNNEL DU CRÉPIEUX APPROACH — DAWN

The city fringe. Warehouses. Concrete flyovers. The sky going from black to old pewter. A light rain has begun — barely mist, just enough to make the road surfaces shine.

The Carlton moves through it at exactly the speed limit. Behind it, two car lengths back — the lead XJR. Further back, across a lane divide — the second XJR.

WIDE — THREE CARS approaching the tunnel mouth. The tunnel entrance is a concrete rectangle cut into the limestone ridge. Two lanes each direction. Sodium orange lights beginning fifty metres in. The far end — a small pale disc of dawn light, six hundred metres away.

CAMERA — GROUND LEVEL at the tunnel mouth as the Carlton passes. The sound changes instantly — the open-air exhaust note becoming something harder, more compressed, bouncing off the curved walls. The sound of an engine inside a stone throat.

INT. LOTUS CARLTON — TUNNEL APPROACH

Cole sees the tunnel mouth coming. Checks the mirror. Lead XJR two cars behind — close enough that he can read the XJR badge in his mirror glass. The second XJR further back.

He rolls his neck once. A small preparation.

COLE(to Nadia)What’s on the other side?
NADIA(from memory — no map needed)Roundabout. Three exits. Right back toward the city. Left under the motorway. Straight — D383 north. Open country in four hundred metres.
COLELength of the tunnel?
NADIASix hundred metres. Maybe six-fifty.

Cole’s eyes go to the speedometer. Sixty km/h.

COLE(quietly, calculating)Thirty-eight seconds at this speed.

He looks at the mirror again. The XJR has closed to one car length. He can see Shaw’s silhouette behind the wheel.

COLE (CONT’D)(earpiece — to Rabbit)Rabbit. Tunnel approach. I’m about to run. Get to the D383 junction by the northern edge of Caluire. Take whatever route gets you there fastest.
RABBIT (V.O.)Already moving. Forty seconds.
COLE(to Nadia)Hands on the dash.

Nadia places both palms flat on the dashboard. She doesn’t ask why. She already knows.

They enter the tunnel.

INT./EXT. TUNNEL DU CRÉPIEUX — CONTINUOUS

The acoustic compression is immediate and total. The Carlton’s exhaust note transforms — the open-air burble becoming a hard, reverberant bark, every cylinder firing bouncing off the orange walls and returning. The road surface is damp. The headlights create two cones of orange-tinged light ahead.

CAMERA — MOUNTED ON THE CARLTON’S BONNET, facing back. The tunnel walls rushing past on both sides. The XJR’s headlights in the distance behind. The ceiling close above.

Cole drives. Steady. Deliberate. Watching the mirror. Counting.

Twenty metres in.

The tunnel curves left — a shallow bend that hides the entrance behind them.

Fifty metres.

The XJR’s headlights are bright in the mirror now. Shaw pushing up.

One hundred metres.

Cole’s right foot moves.

He FLOORS IT.

CAMERA — TIGHT ON THE THROTTLE PEDAL going to the carpet.

The twin turbos — both of them, sequential, the primary spooling first and the secondary catching it in under 0.3 seconds — SLAM into boost simultaneously. There is a fraction of a second of silence, the brief hesitation of physics, and then —

— the Carlton ERUPTS.

377 brake horsepower through the rear wheels. The back end steps out — a quarter-turn of opposite lock from Cole, immediate, unthinking, the correction already applied before the slide begins — the rear tyres find grip on the damp tarmac — and the car ACCELERATES.

Not smoothly. Not gradually. It SURGES — a physical lurch, the g-force pressing both occupants back — the speedometer needle sweeping right like something panicked:

60… 85… 110… 135 km/h.

In four seconds.

CAMERA — LOW, TRACKING alongside: the Carlton’s flanks blurring past the tunnel walls, the gap between car and concrete wall measured in centimetres, the exhaust note inside the bore now a continuous ROAR, a wall of sound, the turbos singing their two-note harmony above it.

The tunnel walls are orange streaks. The far circle of light rushes toward them.

INT. JAGUAR XJR (LEAD) — TUNNEL — CONTINUOUS

Shaw watches the Carlton’s brake lights — they never come on. The car simply ceases to exist at its previous position and reappears as a receding blur.

A tenth of a second of processing.

Then —

SHAWGO —

CAMERA — CLOSE ON Shaw’s right foot stamping the accelerator.

The XJR’s supercharged V8 responds differently to the Carlton — no turbo lag, no spool-up — the supercharger delivers its boost instantaneously, the torque available from the moment the throttle opens. 387 lb-ft of it, arriving NOW.

The automatic transmission — ZF 5-speed — kicks down two ratios. The engine note changes register: the V8 baritone DEEPENS and the supercharger whine RISES, the Eaton blower screaming at the top of its range, a turbine shriek layered over the engine’s thunder.

Inside the tunnel the XJR’s V8 is ENORMOUS. It fills the bore. It comes off both walls. It doubles on itself until it sounds like two engines, three, the tunnel turning it into something almost physical.

140 km/h. The Carlton ahead is pulling away. Thirty metres. Forty.

Shaw’s hands work the wheel through the tunnel’s shallow curve — the XJR is heavier by nearly 400 kilograms, its mass wanting to push wide, Shaw holding it with measured inputs, never fighting, steering with the car not against it.

SHAW (CONT’D)(already reaching for the radio)He’s running — stay left at the roundabout — LEFT —
VINCENT (V.O.)I heard the engine. I’m ready.

The tunnel exit rushes toward them — the pale disc of dawn expanding, filling the windscreen —

INT. LOTUS CARLTON — TUNNEL EXIT — CONTINUOUS

160 km/h as the tunnel mouth opens around them — the sky suddenly ENORMOUS, grey and gold and vast after six hundred metres of orange compression — and Cole is already reading the roundabout.

Three exits. No traffic. A thin mist on the tarmac.

CAMERA — WIDE, from above the roundabout, watching the Carlton arrive.

Cole’s hands move in a single fluid sequence — LEFT hand dropping to the gear lever, RIGHT hand turning the wheel, LEFT foot covering the brake pedal — the Carlton BRAKES hard for 0.8 seconds — the nose dips — the rear loads — then the throttle opens again mid- corner and the power DRIVES the car through the roundabout.

The rear tyres lay a thin black arc across the tarmac — a signature, a mark left by physics — and the Carlton is gone, accelerating north on the D383.

CAMERA — GROUND LEVEL at the roundabout exit. The Carlton’s tail disappearing into the middle distance, taillights becoming dots.

The lead XJR erupts from the tunnel.

Shaw reads the three exits. Makes a decision.

Wrong decision — he goes right — realises — the tyres SHRIEK as he countermands the steering — the XJR fights him, its 1,835 kilograms of mass resisting the change of direction — he overcomes it — finds the correct exit —

Two seconds. Three.

The Carlton is two hundred metres ahead and climbing the hill road north.

Shaw drives. His jaw works. He doesn’t speak.

INT. JAGUAR XJR (LEAD) — POST-TUNNEL

CONNELLY watches the Carlton. A green dot ahead, rising.

CONNELLYHe’s extending.
SHAW(clipped)I see it.
CONNELLYVincent took the left exit.
SHAWGet him on the radio.
CONNELLY(into radio)Vincent — straight exit, D383. You need the underpass, back north —
VINCENT (V.O.)Two minutes.

Shaw pushes the XJR. The supercharger lifts its note as the revs climb — 4,000 RPM — 5,000 — the blower whine rising in pitch, insistent, almost angry. The automatic holds third gear, torque building through the mid-range where this engine lives.

And something in Shaw’s face — something he can’t control — is not entirely anger.

EXT. D383 — NORTH OF THE TUNNEL — DAWN — INTERCUT

The D383 as it leaves the city. A two-lane road rising through the northern suburbs, then opening into farmland. Pale winter wheat on both sides. Stone farmhouses low against a

wind that hasn’t started yet. The Ain river silver in the far distance.

RABBIT in the black Integrale, coming off a side street, the little car settling onto the D383 fifty metres ahead of the junction. He accelerates north.

CAMERA — BEHIND AND LOW on the Integrale. The car is compact, purposeful — wide in the hips from its rally homologation, the fat Speedline alloys filling the arches, the front splitter close to the ground. It moves with a different quality to the Carlton — lighter, more nervous, like a dog that’s been let off the lead.

In the Integrale — Rabbit checks his mirror. Empty. Checks the earpiece.

RABBIT(earpiece)I’m on the D383. Where are you?
COLE (V.O.)Four hundred metres behind you. Don’t slow down.
RABBITAnd them?
COLE (V.O.)Same.

Rabbit nods. Accelerates. The 2.0-litre turbo pulls hard — 210 bhp through four wheels, the Torsen differential reading grip and distributing torque between front and rear axles in real time. The car is planted, confident, entirely at home on a wet road at 150 km/h.

Behind him, the Carlton appears over a rise. Behind the Carlton — the lead XJR.

All three cars, strung out on the D383 like beads on a wire.

ACT THREE: THE OPEN ROAD

EXT. D383 — AIN VALLEY — DAWN

The road north of Lyon straightens and begins to descend toward the valley floor. WIDE ESTABLISHING — the landscape opening up. The limestone country. The river far below catching the morning light. Enormous sky going from pewter to pale gold at the eastern edge.

The Carlton running at 170 km/h, the twin turbos in their element on this straight — not full boost, just the constant surge of mid-range torque, the engine sitting at 3,800 RPM, both turbos spinning, the power smooth and relentless.

The lead XJR 300 metres back and closing.

INT. LOTUS CARLTON — MOVING

Cole drives. Shoulders loose. Eyes far ahead. Nadia has her phone to her ear.

NADIA(lowering the phone)Second car. Coming up the valley road from the west. Intercepts us at the junction in eight minutes if we hold this route.
COLEThey planned this route.
NADIAWhoever’s in that car knows how you think.

Cole’s hands don’t move. His eyes stay on the road. But something shifts in him — the jaw, the flatness settling deeper.

RABBIT (V.O.)(earpiece)I see the western approach road from up here. There’s a vehicle moving fast. Grey Jag. Same model.
COLECopy.
RABBIT (V.O.)Cole. Who are these people?

Long pause. Cole watches the road. The carlton crests a small rise and for a moment — CAMERA LOW AND WIDE — it is silhouetted against the pale gold sky, the valley enormous behind it.

COLEMan named Shaw. We worked together for seven years.
RABBIT (V.O.)And?
COLEAnd then we didn’t.
RABBIT (V.O.)What happened?

Cole looks at the rearview mirror. The XJR is there — a grey shape gaining on the straight, the supercharger note audible now even through closed windows.

COLEHe made a decision. I was the cost of that decision. He decided he could live with that.

Silence on the earpiece. Rabbit processing this.

RABBIT (V.O.)(quietly)Could he?
COLE(very flat)You’d have to ask him.

He downshifts — fourth to third — the engine note rising, the twin turbos building pressure again — and the Carlton surges forward as the road curves left ahead. Cole’s reading the apex from three hundred metres out.

CAMERA — INSIDE, DASHBOARD LEVEL — Cole’s hands on the wheel. The way he turns it: not a sweep, a sequence of small precise inputs, the wheel never more than a quarter-turn from centre, the car guided rather than steered.

INT. JAGUAR XJR (LEAD) — MOVING

Shaw is running 185 km/h. He is closing — but slowly. The Carlton’s lines through the bends are a half-second cleaner than his, the lighter car rotating more quickly, wasting less speed at the apex.

CAMERA — THROUGH THE WINDSCREEN, the Carlton ahead, the green roof visible, the distance between them not quite resolving.

CONNELLYHe’s pulling away.
SHAW(controlled)Not on the straight.

He pushes it. The supercharger climbs its register — the whine building in the cabin, a rising mechanical insistence — as Shaw demands more from the engine. The V8 responds with a surge of torque that doesn’t build so much as arrive, pressing Connelly back into his seat.

190 km/h. The Carlton holds its gap.

CONNELLYShaw —
SHAWI know.
CONNELLYVincent’s coming up from the west —
SHAWI don’t want Vincent to have him.

He stops. Connelly looks at him.

CONNELLY(carefully)You want what?

The road unspools. The valley opens. Shaw drives.

PETRA(from the back, almost gently)You want to talk to him.

Shaw says nothing.

PETRA (CONT’D)That’s not the assignment, Mr. Shaw.
SHAW(quiet, controlled)Tell me what the assignment is, Petra.
PETRAYou know what it is.
SHAW(harder)Say it out loud.

A pause.

PETRAWe take the recon materials. We remove the obstacle to the job.
SHAWRemove.
PETRAYes.

Shaw looks at the Carlton — 280 metres ahead now, taking a long right-hand bend with that terrible precision, the tail placed perfectly on exit, the nose pointing straight before the next input, not a millimetre wasted.

SHAW(almost inaudible)He’s not an obstacle.
CONNELLYShaw —
SHAW(louder, final)He’s not an obstacle. He’s Cole.

Nobody speaks.

The XJR’s engine note fills the silence. The supercharger hums its constant pressurised undertone. Outside — the valley. The river. The cliffs beginning to rise.

Cole brakes. Hard. The nose dives. He steers left — off the tarmac — onto gravel.

INT. JAGUAR XJR (LEAD) — VALLEY FLOOR — CONTINUOUS

Shaw comes off the ridge fast — harder through those bends than Cole, 40 kilograms of extra throttle and slightly wider lines — he’s closed the gap.

Then — the Carlton’s brake lights.

Brief.

Gone.

And the Carlton turns left. Off the road. Onto something.

CONNELLYWhere —

Shaw is already looking. Already processing. He sees the gravel track alongside the river.

SHAWFarm track. Two kilometres north.
CONNELLYThe XJR can’t handle —
SHAW(already braking, already turning)The XJR can do anything I tell it to.

He takes the turn. Too fast for the loose surface. The rear steps out — 1,835 kilograms of

car breaking traction on the gravel — CONNELLY grabs the door handle — Shaw catches it with the steering, a full turn of opposite lock held for two seconds while the tyres bite and find ground beneath the loose stone.

CAMERA — OUTSIDE, BEHIND: the XJR’s rear wheels kicking gravel in a wide rooster-tail arc, the car slewing sideways, then straightening, then MOVING — the V8 digging deep into its torque, the rear wheels hammering the gravel, throwing stones against the underside with a sound like sustained gunfire.

In the cabin — every rock hit transmits directly through the chassis. The suspension was not designed for this. Shaw drives through it, absorbing, adapting.

Gravel hammers the floor, the sills, the undertrays. A larger stone hits the undertray with a sound like a rifle shot.

CONNELLY(through clenched teeth)Lovely.

ACT FOUR: THE TRACK AND THE BRIDGE

EXT. FARM TRACK — RIVER AIN — CONTINUOUS

The Carlton is in its element here — 200 kilograms lighter than the XJR, a lower centre of gravity, narrower — and Cole is threading it through the ruts and loose stone with a rally driver’s eye for line.

CAMERA — VARIOUS ANGLES:

— ABOVE: the Carlton from the air, the river beside it, the cliff wall rising sheer to the right, the car moving through the narrow corridor of the valley —

— BONNET-MOUNTED: the track ahead rushing toward the camera, ruts and rocks and the edge of the river to the left, the cliff face to the right —

— LOW AND SIDE: the rear suspension working — the dampers compressing over rocks, the rear tyres scrabbling for purchase, the differential making constant tiny adjustments —

— THROUGH THE WINDSCREEN: Cole’s eyes. Moving. Always ahead. Reading the surface three seconds before the wheels reach it.

The early morning light between the valley walls is now a single band of amber-gold. The rock face to the right is blazing. The river to the left is grey and fast over pale limestone.

It is genuinely magnificent. Cole has no time for it.

INT. LOTUS CARLTON — FARM TRACK

The ride is brutal — every rut and rock transmitting directly through the firm suspension into the cabin — but Cole reads the surface and minimises the worst of it, picking the smoothest lines, the chassis complying.

His hands never stop moving. Small corrections. The wheel alive.

A section of deep loose gravel — the front end pushes wide — Cole lets it run, reduces throttle to 30%, waits for the front tyres to find the harder substrate beneath — they do — he feeds the power back in smoothly and the car hooks up and drives.

No drama. Just management.

NADIAEight hundred metres to the climb. Fifteen percent grade, then the plateau road.
COLEAnd the second car?
NADIA(looking left, across the river)There. He’s found a bridge.

CAMERA — THROUGH THE PASSENGER WINDOW. Across the river — the second XJR, running parallel on the far bank road. Faster than the Carlton here, the far bank road being tarmac. It’s pulling ahead.

Cole watches it.

COLE(quietly)He’ll be at the bridge before us.
NADIAYes.

A long beat. The track narrows. The river presses in from the left. The cliff presses in from the right.

COLE(earpiece)Rabbit. There’s going to be a blockade at the bridge. Stone arch. End of the track. Can you see it?
RABBIT (V.O.)I’m above it. Looking straight down. One XJR across the approach. One man standing outside.
COLEHow much room to the left of the car?
RABBIT (V.O.)(pause)None. The bridge parapet is right there.
COLERight side?
RABBIT (V.O.)Maybe a metre. Metre and a half. It’s tight, Cole.

Cole drives. The speedometer at 80 km/h — as fast as the track allows.

NADIA(quietly)Can you get through the gap?
COLENo.

A beat.

NADIAThen what?
COLEThen we have a conversation.

INT. JAGUAR XJR (LEAD) — FARM TRACK — CONTINUOUS

Shaw is working hard. The XJR’s wider 1,850mm bodywork keeps catching the edges of the track — the nearside wing brushing the overhanging cliff vegetation, the offside wheels dropping into the gutter at the river edge. He is driving it physically, using his body weight, wrestling the car through a space it was not built for.

CAMERA — INSIDE, SHAW’S HANDS: working the wheel in large rapid inputs, correcting, catching, never quite letting the car settle.

Every rock under the tyres sends a jolt through the structure. The XJR’s suspension — tuned for motorway refinement, not gravel tracks — transmits each one directly.

The Carlton ahead is pulling away. Twenty metres. Thirty.

CONNELLY(braced)He’s losing us.
SHAW(through effort)He’s not losing us.

A rock hits the undertray. Something loud. Something that might be expensive.

SHAW (CONT’D)(radio)Vincent. The track ends at a bridge. Get there first.
VINCENT (V.O.)I’m already across.
SHAWDon’t let him through.

A pause on the radio.

VINCENT (V.O.)And if he doesn’t stop?

Shaw drives. The question hangs.

PETRA(from the back, steady)Then you know what to do.
SHAW(to radio, measured)Vincent. Don’t let him through.

He puts the radio down. His knuckles are white on the wheel.

Connelly watches him.

CONNELLYYou’re going to pull him out of that car.

No response.

CONNELLY (CONT’D)I’ve known you eight years. I know that face.
SHAW(a very long pause)I owe him a conversation.
CONNELLYYou owe him more than that.

Shaw. Silent. Driving.

EXT. BRIDGE — RIVER AIN — CONTINUOUS

A stone arch bridge. Single lane. The river moving fast and grey ten metres below. The second XJR parked sideways across the approach. VINCENT stands outside it — 30s, compact, watchful — arms folded.

The Carlton comes off the final metres of track at 80 km/h, brakes hard — the rear locking momentarily before the ABS catches it — and stops twenty-five metres from the blockade.

Steam rises from the river. The valley is gold and still.

CAMERA — WIDE. The Carlton stopped. The bridge blocked. The cliff on one side, the river on the other. Nowhere to go.

INT. LOTUS CARLTON — STOPPED

The engine ticks. The turbos spin down to idle.

Cole sits completely still. Looking through the windscreen at Vincent and the XJR.

NADIAOne man outside. One in the car.
COLEShaw is ninety seconds behind us.

Silence.

Something moves in Cole’s face — not fear — something older and sadder than fear.

NADIA(carefully)Cole. What do you need?
COLE(pause)I need thirty seconds.

He opens the door.

EXT. BRIDGE APPROACH — CONTINUOUS

Cole walks toward Vincent. Hands open at his sides. His footsteps on the gravel are the only sound in the valley.

Vincent watches him. Doesn’t move.

COLE(stopping ten feet away)Vincent.
VINCENTCole.

A beat.

COLEShaw sends you for his dirty work now.
VINCENTI volunteered.
COLE(studying him)No you didn’t.

Vincent says nothing.

COLE (CONT’D)You like Shaw. You respect Shaw. But you don’t volunteer for this. This isn’t your fight.
VINCENT(quiet)The job is my fight. Someone gets there first, we don’t eat. That’s the fight.
COLEI’m not taking your job. Same bank, different objective, different day.
VINCENTThat’s not what Petra says.
COLE(absorbing this)Petra. Who’s paying Petra?

Vincent says nothing. But something shifts behind his eyes.

COLE (CONT’D)(very precise, very quiet)Someone hired Petra to make sure this doesn’t happen. Not my job. Not yours. Both of them. You and Shaw are being used to clean up someone else’s problem. Nobody told you that.

Gravel crunches behind Cole. He doesn’t turn.

SHAWThat’s a very good story.

EXT. BRIDGE APPROACH — CONTINUOUS

Shaw has stepped out of the XJR. Ten feet behind Cole. Connelly remains in the car.

Three men in the valley light. Shaw. Cole. Vincent.

It is absolutely quiet.

COLE(not turning)Hello, Michael.

A long beat.

SHAWIt’s been a while.
COLE(flat)Four years, three months, eleven days.

Shaw absorbs this.

SHAWI know how long it’s been.
COLE(turning now, slowly)Then you know how I counted it.

They look at each other. Two men who built something together and then didn’t. The damage is there on both of them — different shapes, different locations — but it’s there.

SHAW(quiet)I didn’t have a choice.
COLE(no heat — just precision)Everyone has a choice. Some choices just cost more than others. You decided what you could afford to lose.

A beat.

COLE (CONT’D)You were wrong about what I could afford.

Silence.

SHAW(slowly)I’ve been carrying that.
COLEGood. It should be heavy.

Vincent watches both of them.

PETRA(from the XJR — window cracked)Mr. Shaw. We have a schedule.

Shaw doesn’t look at her.

COLE(quietly, to Shaw only)Who is she working for?

Shaw knows. He’s been choosing not to know.

SHAW(finally)The principle behind the job.
COLEWhy would the principle want the job shut down?
SHAW(realising it as he says it)Because the job was never the job.

Silence.

COLEWe’ve been recon’ing a bank for someone who doesn’t want it robbed. They wanted to know what’s in it.

Shaw looks at Petra’s reflection in the XJR’s window.

SHAW(very quiet)What’s in it, Cole?
COLEI don’t know yet. But it’s enough that someone’s willing to put you against me to find out.

The river. The light. The valley.

Vincent looks at Shaw.

SHAW(to Vincent)Step back from the car.

ACT FIVE: THE PLATEAU

EXT. JURA PLATEAU ROAD — MORNING

Three cars moving north together now.

WIDE ESTABLISHING — the plateau. The high limestone tableland of the Jura massif. The world up here has a different quality — bigger, older, the sky enormous, the horizon distant in every direction. The road a single ribbon of pale tarmac threading between pine forest and limestone cliff.

To the east — the canyon. A void. The limestone dropping three hundred metres to a floor filled with morning mist, the far wall blazing in the new sun.

The Carlton leads. The two XJRs follow.

CAMERA — FROM AHEAD, tracking backward. All three cars in frame — the Carlton in the centre, the XJRs flanking. Three machines on a road that was built for one.

INT. LOTUS CARLTON — MOVING

Cole is driving hard. Not running — leading. Something different in him now. He gives the Carlton its head on a long fast left-hand sweeper.

CAMERA — INSIDE: the G-meter reading 0.8 lateral through the corner. The dashboard vibrating slightly with the engine’s resonance. The boost gauge touching 0.7 bar.

The twin turbos are singing — a high harmonic above the engine’s baritone, both turbos in full flow, the boost pressure building and sustaining through the corner as Cole holds the throttle open. The rear tyres are working — a faint rhythmic shimmy in the steering wheel as the grip varies through the corner.

He catches it. Adjusts. Continues.

NADIA(watching the canyon drop away to the right)My God.

The canyon wall falls away to nothing — three hundred metres of open air, the mist below, the far cliff blazing — and Nadia cannot help it. She looks.

COLE(quietly, to himself)There it is.
NADIAWhat?
COLEThe road. This is why they used this approach. Single file. No way to come at it from two sides. Armoured vehicles — one direction — controlled access.

He looks in the mirror. Shaw’s XJR keeping station, 150 metres back.

COLE (CONT’D)The bank isn’t a bank.

He reaches for the radio.

INT. JAGUAR XJR (LEAD) — MOVING

Shaw drives. The canyon on his right. The road demanding all his attention.

CAMERA — LOW OUTSIDE: the XJR from the passenger side, the canyon wall dropping away behind it, the car three metres from the edge. The supercharger note echoing off the rock face to the left.

CONNELLYHe’s figured it out.
SHAWHe’s been figuring it out since before we got here. I’ve just been catching up.
PETRA(suddenly sharp)Mr. Shaw. Pull alongside. Now.
SHAWWe’re on a one-lane road with a three-hundred-metre drop.
PETRAI’m aware of the geography.
SHAW(even)Then you’re aware I can’t pull alongside him.
PETRA(cold — something has shifted)Then you need to push him off.

The cabin goes quiet.

CONNELLY(slowly turning)What did you say?
PETRAThe contractor’s instructions were clear. The asset in the Carlton has connected —
SHAW(very quietly)Stop talking.
PETRAMr. Shaw, your contract —
SHAW(harder)Stop. Talking.

He looks at the Carlton ahead. The green roof in the morning light. Moving through the canyon like something alive.

He makes a decision.

He picks up the radio.

SHAW (CONT’D)Cole.

INT. LOTUS CARLTON — CONTINUOUS

The radio crackles. Nadia looks at Cole.

COLEI’m here.
SHAW (V.O.)She’s made a call. Backup coming from the north end. Two minutes.
COLEHow many?
SHAW (V.O.)Enough. There’s a junction four hundred metres ahead — left, down to the valley. Take it.

A pause.

COLEWhy?

A longer pause.

SHAW (V.O.)(quietly)Because I’m four years late.

Cole holds the radio. His face is still. But something in him — something that has been hard and closed for a very long time — moves.

He sets the radio down.

He drives.

EXT. JURA CANYON — CONTINUOUS

The Carlton takes the left junction — barely visible, barely a road — and drops away into the tree line below.

The two XJRs continue north.

CAMERA — WIDE, from above. The two grey cars on the plateau road. The canyon ahead. Then — from the north, two vehicles appearing over a ridge. Coming fast.

Shaw doesn’t slow down.

CAMERA — INSIDE SHAW’S XJR: his hands on the wheel. His foot on the accelerator. Connelly looking at him.

The XJR accelerates.

The canyon fills with engine sound — the supercharged V8 and the answering roar of what’s coming — and the limestone walls take it all and throw it back, amplifying it, folding it on itself, until it sounds like something enormous and final.

EXT. VALLEY TRACK — BELOW THE CANYON — SAME TIME

The Carlton descends through the pine trees. The track steep, the surface loose. Cole drives it carefully now. No hurry.

From high above — carried down through the trees — the sound of engines. Rising.

Then a sound that isn’t an engine.

Then silence.

Cole drives. The valley below opens up — silver, wide, clean. The morning fully arrived.

His earpiece crackles.

RABBIT (V.O.)(earpiece — quiet, different tone)Cole.
COLEI’m here.
RABBIT (V.O.)I need you to hear me before you respond.

A beat.

COLEGo ahead.
RABBIT (V.O.)When you told me to hold the plateau — I didn’t hold the plateau.

Nadia looks at Cole.

COLE(very even)Where did you go, Rabbit?
RABBIT (V.O.)Back to the bank.

Silence in the Carlton.

COLEYou went back to the bank.
RABBIT (V.O.)You had two XJRs, a canyon road and whatever Shaw was going to do at the end of it. Nobody was watching the bank. I had the Integrale and forty minutes.

A pause.

RABBIT (V.O.) (CONT’D)I got inside, Cole.
NADIA(barely audible)Christ.
COLE(pause)How far inside?
RABBIT (V.O.)Far enough to see what they’re storing. It’s not money. It’s not bearer bonds. It’s documents. Sealed court records — but not French courts. International tribunal records. War crimes proceedings. Pre-indictment materials. Names. Real names.

Silence in the car.

COLEWhose names?
RABBIT (V.O.)I photographed what I could before the camera rotation came back. I’m still counting. Cole — there are sitting heads of state in those files. Three, maybe four.

The Carlton continues down through the trees. The valley floor approaching.

COLE(very quiet)Are you clear?
RABBIT (V.O.)Back in the car. Nobody saw me. Meet you at the café.

Cole drives. His face is still. But his eyes — in the mirror, in the glass — his eyes are working through something.

NADIA(quietly)If those records reach a tribunal —
COLEThey never will. That’s why the bank exists.

A beat.

NADIAAnd that’s why Petra wanted us out of the picture.
COLEWe weren’t going to rob it. We were going to stumble into it. And whoever put those files there can’t have anyone stumble into them.

The Carlton reaches the valley floor. The road opens up. Cole accelerates — steady, unhurried, north.

EPILOGUE

INT. UNNAMED CAFÉ — BOURG-EN-BRESSE — THREE HOURS LATER

A corner table. Morning fully arrived outside. The street doing normal things — a postman, two women with shopping bags, a man walking a dog that stops at every lamppost. The world.

Cole sits with a coffee. Nadia beside him. Both of them have the contained stillness of people who have metabolised something and are waiting for the next thing.

The café door opens.

RABBIT comes in.

He is 31 years old and looks 28. He has dried mud on his left trouser leg from where the Integrale kicked gravel in the underground car park. He has the look of someone who has been running on adrenaline for four hours and is now, quite suddenly, aware of it.

He sits down across from them.

He puts a small digital camera on the table between them.

They all look at it.

Nobody speaks for a moment.

RABBITThe Integrale is in the car park on the Rue Bichat. Second level. Backed into a corner. Nobody looked twice.
COLEYou said three heads of state.
RABBITMaybe four. The fourth file I only got the cover sheet. The name on it — Cole, I don’t — I’ve never heard of him. But Nadia might.

He slides the camera to Nadia. She turns it on. Scrolls through the photographs. Her face doesn’t change. But she stops on one.

She looks at it for a long time.

NADIA(very quietly)Where did you see this name?
RABBITThird cabinet. Bottom drawer. The file was thick — two inches at least.

Nadia looks at Cole.

COLEWho is it?
NADIA(pause)The man who funded the tribunal that’s supposed to be prosecuting the others.

Silence.

RABBITSo he’s paying for the investigation —
NADIAAnd keeping the evidence that implicates him in the same vault as the evidence against the people he’s prosecuting. In case he ever needs to negotiate.

Cole picks up his coffee. Looks out the window. The postman has finished his round. The women with the shopping bags have gone around a corner. The man with the dog has sat on a bench.

COLEWe were hired to map the approach to a building.
NADIAYes.
COLEAnd instead we found what’s inside it.

He looks at the camera on the table.

COLE (CONT’D)(to Nadia)Who do we take this to?
NADIA(a long pause)That depends on what we want to happen next.

A beat.

RABBIT(looking between them)What do we want to happen next?

Cole looks at him. Then at Nadia. Then at the window.

COLEWe finish the job we were hired to do. We complete the recon. We deliver the approach plan to whoever’s paying us.

A beat.

COLE (CONT’D)And then we keep a copy of those photographs.

Silence.

RABBITFor what?

Cole picks up his coffee.

COLE(very quiet)For the day we need to negotiate.

He drinks.

The sun comes through the window and crosses the table. The camera sits between them, small and ordinary, containing something that is neither.

Outside, the street does what streets do. The world goes on being exactly what it is — which is to say, more complicated than it looks.

FADE TO BLACK.
