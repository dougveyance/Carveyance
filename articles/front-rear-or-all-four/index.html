Drivetrains, Explained

Front, Rear, or All Four?

A field guide to FWD, RWD, and AWD — told through a Citroën, a BMW, and a Subaru. Three ways to move a car, three ways to feel alive, and not one of them is the "right" answer.

By Douglas Sellers ◆ Carveyance ◆ 22 min read

Think about the great movie trilogies for a second.

Take the one that spawned a xenomorph. Alien is a horror film — slow, claustrophobic, terrifying. Aliens is a full-blooded action-thriller — louder, faster, guns everywhere. Alien 3 is a bleak, stripped-down drama that trades the fireworks for dread. Same universe. Same creature. Same DNA. Three completely different films — and fans have argued about which one is "best" for decades precisely because there is no correct answer. They were never trying to be the same movie. Each one is brilliant at being exactly what it is.

Drivetrains work the same way.

Front-wheel drive, rear-wheel drive, and all-wheel drive are three versions of the same idea — get power to the ground and move the car — and each one has a completely different personality. One is not the sequel that improved on the others. They are siblings, not rankings. And the fight over which is "best" has kept enthusiasts happily arguing at Cars & Coffee for about as long as cars have existed.

Because a car is not just an engine, a horsepower number, a 0–60 time, or an increasingly enormous screen asking whether you'd like to update your subscription to heated seat warmth. Its personality begins with a much simpler question:

Which wheels are doing the work?

That one question changes almost everything. How the car accelerates. How it corners. How it behaves in the rain. How it feels on a mountain road. How much room it has inside. How brave you feel in February. How much trouble you can get into leaving a car meet.

So let's break it down properly, using three cars that each define their layout. For rear-wheel drive, the BMW 3 Series — the sports-sedan teacher who wears driving gloves and quietly judges your corner entry. For all-wheel drive, the Subaru Impreza — the snowy-road Labrador of the car world: loyal, eager, muddy, and somehow always ready. And for front-wheel drive, we'll go French, naturally, with the Citroën Traction Avant — one of the cars that made front-wheel drive respectable in the first place.

But first, the origin story.

Who Actually Invented Front-Wheel Drive?

Short answer: nobody you'd expect, and not who usually gets the credit. Front-wheel drive wasn't a single lightbulb moment — it was a slow crawl toward respectability, with a few brave failures along the way. Britain's Alvis was building front-drive cars by 1928. Germany's DKW put the layout into small, affordable cars in the early 1930s. And in America, the Cord L-29 arrived in 1929 as the first successful U.S. production front-wheel-drive car — a spectacular Art Deco experiment from the glamorous Auburn-Cord-Duesenberg universe.

The L-29 was beautiful, low, dramatic, and technically fascinating. It was also expensive, launched straight into the teeth of the Great Depression, and gone from production by 1932. It proved front-wheel drive could exist in a luxurious production car. It did not prove it could work for everyone.

That job fell to a nearly bankrupt French company and a car unveiled in Paris on April 18, 1934.

The Citroën Traction Avant — the name literally means "front traction," i.e. front-wheel drive — was not the first FWD car. But it was the one that changed the argument. Citroën bundled front-wheel drive together with a welded steel monocoque body, hydraulic brakes, and independent suspension on all four wheels, making it the world's first front-wheel-drive steel monocoque production car. Nicknamed "the car with 100 patents," it stayed in production until 1957 and racked up roughly 760,000 sales. It looked, drove, and held the road like the future had arrived wearing a beret. So the short version is this:

The FWD timeline, sorted

Early pioneers who proved it was possible: Alvis (UK, 1928), Cord L-29 (USA, 1929), DKW (Germany, early 1930s).

The car that made it credible and mainstream: the Citroën Traction Avant (1934) — the first FWD steel monocoque put into real mass production.

The layout most of the world's economy cars eventually adopted: front-wheel drive.

Now let's get to the fun part.

Front-Wheel Drive — The Citroën Traction Avant

Front-wheel drive means the engine sends power to the front wheels — the same wheels that also steer the car. Which sounds simple, right up until you realize those front tires are now holding down three jobs at once: steering, putting the power down, and carrying much of the engine's weight. The front wheels are essentially the waiter, the chef, the bartender, and the accountant, all at the same table. No wonder they occasionally complain.

How It Works

In a front-wheel-drive car, the front tires pull the whole car down the road — think of a dog pulling a sled, except the dog is also choosing the direction and occasionally deciding whether physics is optional. The Traction Avant made this arrangement seem sophisticated rather than strange. Low body, modern construction, and — for its era — genuinely excellent road holding. That mattered enormously, because most cars of the day were tall, crude, body-on-frame, and rear-driven. Citroën made front-wheel drive look like progress.

How It Feels

Front-wheel drive feels secure, practical, and easy to understand. The nose of the car feels like it's pulling you forward, and in rain or light snow that's reassuring, because the engine's weight sits right over the driven wheels and helps them find grip when you pull away. It's efficient. It packages beautifully — no big driveshaft tunnel running to the rear axle means more room inside. And it's usually cheaper and lighter to build. That's the sensible part.

The fun part is that a good FWD car can be absolutely brilliant, and French manufacturers understood this better than almost anyone. Citroën gave us the Traction Avant; Peugeot later gave us the 205 GTI; Renault gave us a long line of deranged hot Clios. Somehow the French took the most practical drivetrain layout in the world and turned it into something that could dance through a roundabout like it had just insulted a German engineer.

A great FWD car isn't about smoky drifts. It's about momentum. You brake neatly, turn in cleanly, keep the nose loaded, and let the front tires pull you out the other side. Overdrive it, and the car will usually understeer — the front tires run wide and the car calmly informs you, "Non, monsieur, we are going straight now."

FWD, in a Nutshell

  • ✓ Best at: rainy cities, snowy suburbs (with good tires), efficient commuting, interior space, and affordable performance.
  • ✗ Weak at: big-power launches, perfect balance, rear-end playfulness, and being asked to do too much through two front tires.

Front-wheel drive is the friend who shows up on time, brings snacks, does the taxes correctly — and then surprises everyone by being hilarious after two glasses of wine.

Rear-Wheel Drive — The BMW 3 Series

Rear-wheel drive means the engine sends power to the rear wheels while the front wheels handle the steering. This is the classic enthusiast layout, and its whole trick is the division of labor: the front tires steer, the rear tires push, everyone has exactly one job, and nobody is doing the accounting while also cooking the fish.

How It Works

The BMW 3 Series is the perfect classroom example, because for decades it basically was the idea of the compact rear-wheel-drive sports sedan. From the very first 3 Series in 1975, BMW built the car around sporty handling, precise steering, strong brakes, and chassis finesse — and, crucially, steering that stays clean because it isn't corrupted by torque steer.

That phrase — steering uncorrupted by torque steer — is the whole point. In a powerful front-wheel-drive car, the same wheels steer and pull, so under hard acceleration the steering wheel can tug left or right in your hands. That's torque steer: the car reminding you that its front tires are overworked and possibly unionizing. In a rear-drive 3 Series, the front wheels are freed from acceleration duty entirely. They get to concentrate on steering, which is exactly why a good 3 Series has always felt so clean, balanced, and direct.

How It Feels

Rear-wheel drive feels natural when you drive it hard. The car pushes from behind, the front end feels lighter and more precise, and you can adjust the car's attitude with the throttle — add a little power mid-corner and the rear settles and squats; add too much, especially in the wet, and the rear starts to move. That's where RWD gets its reputation, for better and worse.

A good 3 Series on a good road feels like the car is rotating around you. The steering talks, the nose points, the rear axle joins the conversation. In a great one — an E30, an E46, or a well-sorted modern car — it feels like it wants to work with you rather than merely obey you. That's why enthusiasts love it.

But let's be honest: rear-wheel drive in snow can turn even a confident person into a philosopher. With bad tires and worse judgment, a RWD car in winter becomes an expensive sled with heated seats. With proper winter tires, some weight over the back, and a little restraint, it's perfectly usable — it just asks more of you than FWD or AWD ever will.

RWD, in a Nutshell

  • ✓ Best at: dry-road handling, steering feel, balance, sporty driving, and track days and mountain roads.
  • ✗ Weak at: snow and ice without proper tires, packaging efficiency, low-grip launches, and drivers who confuse confidence with talent.

Rear-wheel drive is the charming friend who makes dinner exciting, tells brilliant stories — and then tries to powerslide out of the restaurant parking lot.

Who Actually Invented All-Wheel Drive?

Rear-wheel drive, you'll notice, never got an origin story in this piece — and that's rather the point. Nobody invented it. It's simply how the automobile was born; the earliest cars sent their power rearward because that was the obvious way to do it. All-wheel drive, like front-wheel drive, is a layout that had to be dragged into existence — and its history is older and stranger than most people assume.

It starts in 1903, with a Dutch racing car called the Spyker 60 HP. Nicknamed "the car of three firsts," it was the world's first petrol-powered four-wheel-drive car, the first with a six-cylinder engine, and the first with brakes on all four wheels — built to survive the brutal, unpaved roads of the 1903 Paris–Madrid race. It was wildly ahead of its time, which is a polite way of saying almost nobody followed its lead. For the next half-century, four driven wheels mostly meant one thing: going places cars weren't supposed to go.

That's the Jeep era. World War II made four-wheel drive famous for climbing mountains, fording rivers, and winning wars — not for cornering or riding nicely. Which is exactly why the next car matters so much.

In 1966, the British firm Jensen built the Jensen FF, and it did for all-wheel drive roughly what the Traction Avant did for front-wheel drive. It was the first proper road car — a luxury grand tourer — built around four-wheel drive (the "FF" stands for Ferguson Formula, the system beneath it). For good measure, it was also the first production car in the world with anti-lock brakes. It was expensive, complicated, and sold in tiny numbers, but it proved four-wheel drive could belong to a fast, civilised car rather than a farm implement. And here's the detail that ties this whole story together: when Audi set out to build the Quattro years later, they reportedly bought a used Jensen FF, took its drivetrain apart, and studied exactly how it worked.

Then two companies turned that idea into an identity. In 1972, Subaru launched the Leone — an ordinary, affordable four-wheel-drive wagon that quietly began the obsession that would eventually define the entire brand (and, decades later, give us our Impreza). And in 1980, Audi unveiled the Quattro, which dragged all-wheel drive from "useful" all the way to "thrilling," rewrote rallying, and made the rest of the industry realise that four driven wheels could be a performance weapon and not merely a foul-weather insurance policy. Hold that thought about the Audi. It has one more argument to settle before we're done.

The AWD timeline, sorted

The pioneer who proved it was possible: the Spyker 60 HP (1903) — the world's first petrol four-wheel-drive car.

The era that made it rugged and famous: the Jeep and its military kin (WWII) — traction for going absolutely anywhere.

The car that made it credible for the road: the Jensen FF (1966) — the first four-wheel-drive grand tourer, and the first car of any kind with anti-lock brakes.

The ones that made it an identity: Subaru (from the 1972 Leone onward) and the Audi Quattro (1980), which made all-wheel drive genuinely desirable.

All-Wheel Drive — The Subaru Impreza

All-wheel drive means the engine sends power to all four wheels. This is not magic. AWD does not repeal the laws of physics, it does not make ice disappear, and it certainly does not make bald tires acceptable. What it does do is help the car use more of the grip that's actually available when you're accelerating. And for that, our hero is the Subaru Impreza.

How It Works

Subaru has made all-wheel drive the center of its entire identity, and every Impreza still comes with standard Symmetrical All-Wheel Drive. The system constantly evaluates acceleration, braking, and cornering in real time and instantly shuffles power toward the wheels that have the most grip the moment a tire starts to slip. The flat "boxer" engine sits low, the driveline runs in a straight, symmetrical path down the middle of the car, and the whole layout is built around balance. An Impreza isn't the fastest thing on the road, but in rain, snow, gravel, or genuinely awful weather it has the energy of a car that woke up and chose usefulness.

How It Feels

AWD feels planted. Where FWD pulls and RWD pushes, AWD digs in — and you notice it most when you're accelerating out of a slippery corner or clawing up a wet hill. Instead of asking two tires to do everything, the car quietly spreads the work across four. Press the throttle on a wet road in an Impreza and it doesn't panic; it finds grip and gets on with things. It gives you the sense that while other cars are still negotiating with the road, the Subaru has already brought a shovel, a dog, a thermos, and a plan.

But AWD has real trade-offs. It adds weight. It adds mechanical complexity. It can dent your fuel economy compared with a simpler two-wheel-drive layout. And because it gives you so much traction when accelerating, it can quietly make drivers overconfident — which brings us to the single most important thing to understand about it. AWD helps you go. It does absolutely nothing extra to help you stop. Braking still comes down to your tires, your brakes, the road surface, and physics, who remains undefeated.

AWD, in a Nutshell

  • ✓ Best at: rain, snow, gravel, mountain climates, all-weather confidence, and escaping slippery driveways.
  • ✗ Weak at: extra weight, added complexity, fuel economy, false confidence, and people who say "I have AWD" while driving on terrible tires.

All-wheel drive is the friend who brings extra jackets, jumper cables, snacks, and somehow already knows how to get the fire started.

What Happens When Grip Runs Out

Every layout has a personality at the limit, too — a way it behaves the moment you ask for more grip than the tires can supply. Here's the simple version.

FWD tends to understeer. Push it too hard and the car wants to continue straight instead of turning — the nose runs wide. It's generally safer and more predictable for the average driver, which is a big reason manufacturers like it.

RWD can oversteer. The rear of the car rotates. In skilled hands, that feels alive and adjustable and wonderful. In careless hands, it becomes a YouTube video filmed vertically.

AWD feels planted — but it is not invincible. It can feel secure and fast, yet plenty of AWD cars still understeer when pushed, because manufacturers deliberately tune them for safety. Grip at all four corners is a huge advantage. It is not a force field.

Which One Works Best, and Where?

Geography changes the answer more than horsepower ever will. Where you actually drive — the weather, the roads, the season — quietly decides which layout makes your life easier.

Southern CaliforniaBest: RWD or FWD
In L.A., San Diego, or Orange County, you simply don't need AWD unless you're regularly headed to the mountains. Dry roads let a 3 Series shine, and a good FWD car is perfectly practical and efficient. AWD is nice, but rarely necessary — unless you ski, snowboard, camp, or enjoy pretending the Whole Foods parking lot is a rally stage.
Pacific NorthwestBest: AWD or FWD
Rain changes everything. An Impreza starts making a lot of sense in Seattle or Portland, or anywhere the roads are wet more often than your socks. FWD is strong here too — stable and efficient. RWD can still be great, but a good RWD car on proper tires beats an AWD car on old rubber. Tire quality is the plot twist in nearly every drivetrain argument.
Colorado, Tahoe, Vermont, Upstate NYBest: AWD + winter tires
This is Subaru country. An Impreza on good winter tires is like a mountain goat with heated mirrors — it won't make you immortal, but it makes winter driving far less dramatic. FWD on winter tires can also be excellent. RWD can work, but it demands more skill, more patience, and often more humility.
FloridaBest: FWD
Flat roads, sudden rainstorms, heat, and traffic. FWD makes sense — efficient, practical, stable in a downpour. AWD isn't harmful, just rarely essential. RWD is fun, but Florida isn't exactly overflowing with alpine switchbacks unless you count aggressive parking-garage ramps.
The MidwestBest: FWD or AWD
For daily life, FWD is simple and effective. For snowbelt areas, AWD adds real confidence. RWD can be enjoyable, but winter tires are not optional if you'd like to remain pointed in the direction you originally intended.

So Which One Is Best?

There is no universal winner. That is the whole point. A drivetrain isn't just an engineering layout — it's a personality type, and the smartest way to see it is as three overlapping circles rather than a leaderboard. Each one owns a corner of the map. They share more than the arguments online would suggest. And right in the middle, where all three overlap, sits the thing that actually decides how any car drives.

Front-wheel drive is clever and practical. It gives you interior space, good bad-weather traction, and low-cost usability. It democratized transportation — it is the people's layout. It's not always glamorous, but a good FWD car has real charm: the Traction Avant proved the concept could be sophisticated, and later French cars proved it could be a riot. Feels like: being pulled confidently by the nose.

Rear-wheel drive is the enthusiast's layout. It hands you balance, steering clarity, and the ability to use the throttle as part of the handling. It's not always the most practical, but it often feels the most natural when you're driving purely for pleasure. The 3 Series became famous because it understood that. It didn't just move — it responded. Feels like: being pushed from behind while the front tires write poetry.

All-wheel drive is confidence. It gives you traction, stability, and security when the weather turns hostile. It isn't automatically more fun, and it doesn't erase the need for good tires, but in the right climate it can feel like a superpower. The Impreza is the perfect example because, for Subaru, AWD was never an afterthought — it's the whole identity. Feels like: the car has claws.

The Big Secret: Tires Matter More Than People Admit

Here's the part that quietly ruins most online arguments. A FWD car on excellent winter tires will routinely beat an AWD car on bad all-seasons in the snow. A RWD BMW on proper performance tires will feel sharper than an AWD car on mediocre rubber. And a Subaru Impreza on quality winter tires turns winter from a crisis into a mild inconvenience.

The drivetrain decides where the power goes. The tires decide whether any of that matters. Or, to put it another way: arguing about AWD vs. FWD vs. RWD while ignoring your tires is like arguing about running shoes while wearing socks made of butter.

Final Thought: Three Ways to Move a Car

The real beauty of this whole subject is that all three answers are right — depending on the car, the road, the weather, and the person behind the wheel. The Citroën Traction Avant reminds us that front-wheel drive was once genuinely revolutionary; it pulled the car into the modern age and made it lower, roomier, more efficient, and more usable. The BMW 3 Series reminds us why rear-wheel drive became sacred to enthusiasts; it separates steering from propulsion and hands the driver that delicious sense of balance, rotation, and control. And the Subaru Impreza reminds us that all-wheel drive isn't just for rally stages and ski towns; it's confidence made mechanical, especially when the road turns wet, loose, white, or unpleasant.

FWD pulls. RWD pushes. AWD digs.

And somewhere between those three simple ideas is almost the entire story of how cars feel.

Sidebar

Drivetrain Myths That Need to Die

Every enthusiast has heard them. They tend to appear at Cars & Coffee, in comment sections, or from a man leaning on a fender who begins every sentence with "Actually…" Some of these myths are harmless. Others are expensive. A few are the reason ditches exist. So let's clear the garage floor.

Myth 1: AWD Means You Can Stop Faster

No. Absolutely not. Please stop telling people this before Subaru owners start braking later than physics allows. All-wheel drive helps a car accelerate by feeding power to more than two wheels — that's its great party trick. It helps you pull away from a snowy driveway, climb a wet hill, or launch out of a gravel corner with less drama. But the moment you hit the brakes, every car is already using all four wheels. A front-drive Citroën, a rear-drive 3 Series, and an all-wheel-drive Impreza all brake on four contact patches. AWD doesn't give you four extra tires. It just gives you four driven ones. Stopping comes down to tires, brakes, weight, road surface, and reaction time.

AWD helps you go. Tires help you stop. Confidence helps you crash if you misunderstand the first two.

Myth 2: FWD Is Boring

Front-wheel drive is not boring. Bad front-wheel-drive cars are boring. A good FWD car is all about momentum, precision, and commitment — you manage the nose, keep the front tires loaded, and let the car pull itself through the corner. It rewards neatness and punishes clumsy overdriving. The Traction Avant made front-wheel drive sophisticated; later French hot hatches made it hilarious. A great FWD car doesn't slide around like a rear-drive BMW, but that doesn't mean it lacks personality — it just speaks a different language.

RWD says, "Let's rotate." AWD says, "I've got grip." FWD says, "Carry your speed properly, you animal."

Myth 3: RWD Is Useless in Snow

Rear-wheel drive is not useless in snow. Rear-wheel drive on bad tires is useless in snow. There's a difference. A 3 Series on proper winter tires is perfectly manageable in the cold — it still asks more of you than a FWD or AWD car, especially pulling away uphill or on ice, but it is nowhere near hopeless. The real problem is that people buy a rear-drive car, leave summer tires on it, see a single snowflake, and then act astonished when the car pirouettes like it's been accepted into ballet school.

A RWD car with winter tires is a vehicle. A RWD car with summer tires in snow is a lawsuit with Bluetooth.

Myth 4: AWD Makes You Invincible

AWD gives you traction. It does not give you judgment. This is the great Subaru Impreza lesson. Yes, all-wheel drive helps enormously in rain, snow, gravel, and nasty weather, and it can make a car feel planted, secure, and wonderfully capable. But that same confidence can become a trap. Because AWD makes it easier to accelerate, drivers start assuming they also have more cornering grip and braking grip than they really do. They do not. The car might launch beautifully from a snowy stop sign — but if you enter the next corner too fast, AWD will not negotiate with the guardrail on your behalf.

AWD is a tool. Not a cape.

Myth 5: FWD Can't Be Fast

Tell that to decades of hot hatches. Front-wheel drive does have limits, especially as power climbs — push too much horsepower through the front wheels and you'll meet torque steer, wheelspin, and understeer. Those front tires are already steering, carrying weight, and doing much of the braking; asking them to handle huge power on top of all that is like asking one person to run the restaurant, cook the food, park the cars, and personally apologize to Yelp. But within reason, FWD can be brilliantly quick. It's light, compact, efficient, and superb at putting usable power down in normal conditions. A well-sorted FWD car can embarrass much more powerful machinery on a tight road, because it's easy to place, forgiving, and eager. FWD isn't slow. It's just honest about when you've asked too much of the front tires.

Myth 6: RWD Is Always Best for Enthusiasts

Rear-wheel drive is wonderful. It is not automatically superior. A great RWD car can feel balanced, adjustable, and pure — that's exactly why the 3 Series was the benchmark for so long. But layout alone doesn't make a car great. A bad RWD car is still bad: a poorly tuned chassis, numb steering, cheap tires, too much weight, or sloppy suspension will ruin the party regardless of which wheels are driven. Meanwhile, a brilliant FWD or AWD car can feel more alive than a mediocre rear-driver.

The badge on the trunk doesn't matter. The layout doesn't matter by itself. The execution matters.

Myth 7: AWD Is Always Better Than FWD

Not necessarily. AWD is better in specific situations — snow, gravel, steep wet roads, poor traction, and hard launches. But it also adds weight, cost, complexity, and often fuel consumption. For someone in Los Angeles, Phoenix, Miami, or Dallas, AWD may be nice but genuinely unnecessary, and a good FWD car on quality tires will be cheaper, lighter, more efficient, and easier to maintain. For someone in Tahoe, Vermont, Colorado, or the Pacific Northwest, AWD starts making a much stronger case. The best drivetrain depends on where you live.

AWD in the mountains makes sense. AWD for dry freeway commuting is often just emotional support hardware.

Myth 8: Understeer Is Safe and Oversteer Is Dangerous

This one is half true, which somehow makes it worse. Understeer is when the front tires lose grip and the car runs wider than you intended. Manufacturers deliberately tune everyday cars to understeer because it's predictable, and the average driver's instinct — lift off the throttle and slow down — usually helps. Oversteer is when the rear tires lose grip and the back steps out; it's a delight if you know what you're doing and terrifying if you don't. But understeer is not "safe" if you're understeering into a tree, and oversteer is not automatically dangerous if the driver has skill, space, and control. The real problem was never understeer or oversteer. The real problem is arriving at a corner with more enthusiasm than talent.

Myth 9: Tires Matter Less If You Have AWD

This is the big one. Tires are everything. A Subaru Impreza on bald tires is not a winter weapon — it's a very confident sled. A 3 Series on proper winter tires can out-brake and out-corner an AWD car on bad all-seasons. A FWD car on quality tires can be superb in rain and light snow. The tires are the only part of the car actually touching the road — not the engine, not the badge, not the drivetrain layout, not your confidence, and definitely not the salesman's promise that "this thing is amazing in the snow." Four tiny contact patches decide everything. Ignore them and the road will educate you.

Myth 10: Drivetrain Determines the Whole Personality of the Car

Drivetrain matters enormously — but it's only one ingredient in the recipe. Suspension tuning matters. Weight distribution matters. Tires matter. Steering matters. Wheelbase, chassis stiffness, driver position, even throttle response — all of it matters. A French FWD hatchback can feel more playful than a heavy rear-drive luxury coupe. An Impreza can feel more secure than a powerful rear-drive sedan. A 3 Series can feel more delicate and communicative than cars that are far faster. The drivetrain tells you where the power goes. It does not tell you the whole story of the car. That's why we actually drive them.

Myth 11: Rally Proves One Drivetrain Is Always Best

Rally history is the perfect place to destroy simple answers — so let's finish there. Front-wheel drive had already punched miles above its weight long before anyone said "Group B." The Mini Cooper S humiliated larger, more powerful cars at the Monte Carlo Rally, winning outright in 1964, 1965, and 1967. (It actually locked out the top three in 1966 as well, only for French officials to disqualify all of them over a debatable headlight technicality — rallying has always been a little unfair.) That tiny front-drive box was supposed to carry groceries, small children, and a deeply suspicious amount of cheese. Instead, it became a legend.

Then Group B changed the conversation entirely. By the early 1980s, Audi turned up with the quattro and walked into rallying like a German engineer had discovered witchcraft: four driven wheels, turbocharged five-cylinder noise, and traction that made loose surfaces something to attack rather than merely survive. Audi took the 1982 manufacturers' world title, and in 1983 Hannu Mikkola and Arne Hertz became the first World Rally champions in a four-wheel-drive car. So that was that, right? AWD wins, everyone go home, physics has spoken.

Not quite. Because then came Lancia.

The Lancia 037 was rear-wheel drive. On paper it should have been obsolete the instant the quattro appeared — Audi had traction, technology, and brute force. Lancia had lightness, balance, strategy, Walter Röhrl, Markku Alén, Italian cunning, and what can only be described as competitive stubbornness at a national level. The 1983 World Rally Championship became one of motorsport's great drivetrain arguments: Audi's quattro was the obvious future, and Lancia's 037 was the past politely refusing to leave the restaurant. And somehow, Lancia won the manufacturers' championship — by just two points.

Mikkola's Audi took the drivers' title, so the season split in the most delicious way imaginable: Audi proved the future in the drivers' fight, while Lancia stole the manufacturers' crown with the last rear-wheel-drive car ever to do it. They even made a film about it — Race for Glory: Audi vs. Lancia, released in 2024, dramatizing that 1983 showdown. The lesson? Rally doesn't prove one drivetrain is always best. It proves that the right car, on the right surface, with the right driver, the right tires, the right strategy, and the right amount of madness can make almost anything look like genius.

The Mini pulled. The Lancia pushed. The Audi dug.

And rally fans have been arguing happily ever since.

The Final Truth

FWD pulls, RWD pushes, AWD digs — but the real magic was never simply about which wheels are driven. It's about how the whole car comes together. The Citroën Traction Avant proved front-wheel drive could be clever, modern, and elegant. The BMW 3 Series proved rear-wheel drive could make a sedan feel like a dance partner. The Subaru Impreza proved all-wheel drive could turn terrible weather into a mild inconvenience.

The myth is that one layout is always best. The truth is far better: the best drivetrain is the one that fits the road, the weather, the car, and the kind of driver you want to be. Three siblings. Three genres. No wrong answer — just the right one for you.
